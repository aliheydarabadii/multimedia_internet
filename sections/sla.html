Efficient Link Sharing & QoS Guarantees

In modern IP networks, multiple types of applications‚Äîsuch as voice, video, and data‚Äîmust coexist and share the same links. The goal of link sharing is twofold: to maximize efficiency and to guarantee Quality of Service (QoS) for each application type.

Media-Oriented Applications

Media-oriented applications (like video calls or live event streaming) often have strict bandwidth needs. For example:

  • Telephony: ~100 Kbit/s
  • High-quality live video: >10 Mbit/s (depending on codec used)

These applications benefit from clearly defined resource allocation to ensure smooth performance.

Data Applications

In contrast, data applications (like file transfers or cloud storage) are bandwidth-hungry. They attempt to consume as much bandwidth as available, which can potentially affect the QoS of other services if not properly managed.

To ensure a balanced experience, modern networks must carefully manage how links are shared‚Äîrespecting the critical bandwidth needs of delay-sensitive apps while optimizing for the aggressive nature of data applications.

The Infinite Bandwidth Myth & Resource Management

At first glance, high-capacity optical fiber networks give the impression of limitless bandwidth. In theory, this should eliminate the need for managing how links are shared. However, real-world experience shows that even with such advanced infrastructure, network bottlenecks still occur.

The real challenge is not the raw bandwidth, but how it's allocated and shared. Poor resource management can lead to congestion, poor Quality of Service (QoS), and inefficient link usage‚Äîeven on powerful networks.

That's why Part 1 of this course emphasizes the importance of resource management. It sets the stage for understanding how networks operate and serve different types of applications under constraints.

Understanding QoS from the Customer‚ÄìProvider Perspective

This section explores how Quality of Service (QoS) is tied to the relationship between users and network providers in a multimedia transport network.

There are two main customer types:

  • üßç
    Retail Customers typically use best-effort Internet services. That means there's no guaranteed bandwidth, delay, or delivery quality. It's a "we'll do our best" scenario.
  • üßë‚Äçüíº
    Business Customers, however, can negotiate Service Level Agreements (SLAs). This allows them to request specific performance guarantees for their applications (e.g., low-latency video conferencing, uninterrupted streaming, etc.).

This distinction is important when designing or analyzing network services, especially for applications that require consistent quality, such as voice or video streaming.

Olympic Services: A Real-World SLA Model for QoS

In the world of Quality of Service (QoS), Service Level Agreements (SLAs) play a vital role in setting performance expectations. A simple and widely adopted model is known as Olympic Services, which categorizes service guarantees into Gold, Silver, and Bronze tiers.

Each tier defines:

  • d: Maximum allowable delay
  • p: The probability of violating that delay
Tier Max Delay Violation Probability
Gold 90 ms 0.001 (0.1%)
Silver 120 ms 0.001 (0.1%)
Bronze 250 ms 0.001 (0.1%)

This model ensures that critical applications (e.g., voice or video) can select a suitable service level based on delay sensitivity, while maintaining fairness across the network.

Key Formula: An SLA is expressed as \(SLA = (d, p)\), meaning a delay bound with an acceptable violation probability.

Understanding Delay Distribution in QoS

To ensure Quality of Service (QoS), network delay must be modeled as a random variable. This means that each packet's delay is not fixed, but varies depending on traffic and network conditions.

A Service Level Agreement (SLA) sets a delay threshold \(d\) and an acceptable violation probability \(p\). Formally, this is expressed as:

\[ P(D > d) = \int_{d}^{\infty} f_D(t) dt \le p \]

Where:

  • \(f_D(t)\) is the probability density function of the delay
  • \(E[D]\) is the average (expected) delay

The shaded red area under the curve represents the probability of delay exceeding the threshold.

This concept helps ensure that services‚Äîespecially those needing real-time communication‚Äîcan function reliably within specified delay constraints.

Key Insight: QoS is not just about averages. It's about how often the delay exceeds a certain bound‚Äîthis is what users notice.

Measuring SLAs: Client vs. Telco Perspective

One of the most important aspects of a Service Level Agreement (SLA) is not just defining it, but also measuring it accurately.

  • The Client must be able to measure the actual service received to verify that the SLA terms are met.
  • The Contract must:
    • Clearly define the SLA metrics (e.g., delay, packet loss)
    • Specify how the metrics are measured
    • Include the penalties or compensations if the agreed thresholds are violated

This introduces a practical challenge:

Who measures the performance ‚Äî the client or the provider?

This is where Client vs. Telco dynamics come into play:

  • Clients need independent monitoring tools to avoid relying solely on the provider's metrics.
  • Telcos (network providers) may offer built-in metrics but could have a conflict of interest.

Best practice: Use neutral, auditable measurement systems and clarify the measurement method in the SLA from the start.

Measuring SLA Delay: A Proposal from the Telco to the Client

To verify that the agreed Service Level Agreement (SLA) is being respected, network providers (telcos) may suggest a standardized measurement process.

üîß Telco's Step-by-Step Proposal:

  1. Start with an SLA defined by a delay threshold \(d\) and an acceptable violation probability \(p\).
  2. Send a test packet every \(\Delta t\) seconds.
  3. Measure delay \(D_i\) for each packet \(i\) ‚Äî this is the time it takes for the packet to travel from sender to receiver.
  4. At the end of the observation period, collect the delays of all \(N\) packets.
  5. Compute the average delay using the formula: \[ \text{Average Delay} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} D_i \] This gives the mean delay across all test packets.
  6. Compare the result to the SLA:
    • If the average delay exceeds the threshold \(d\), then the SLA has been violated.

SLA Violation Dispute: When Clients Push Back

Sometimes, clients may challenge the way SLA (Service Level Agreement) violations are defined and measured. In response, the telco proposes a more flexible approach to reduce the chance of false SLA violation claims.

üìë Updated Proposal from the Telco:

Keeping steps 1‚Äì4 from the previous proposal, the new SLA calculation includes two changes:

  • Trim the top 10% of highest delay samples:
    • These are considered outliers that may not reflect typical performance.
    • Helps avoid penalizing providers for rare spikes.
  • Cancel delay samples taken during known congestion events:
    • If a delay is recorded during a moment of network congestion (e.g., router overload), that data point is excluded.
    • This avoids attributing blame unfairly when the network is temporarily overwhelmed.

‚ö†Ô∏è Criticism:

  • These steps make it harder to claim that the SLA has been legally violated.
  • While they protect the provider, they can weaken client protections if not clearly agreed upon.

A Better Way to Measure SLA Violations

Instead of calculating the average delay or removing outliers, a more robust and fair method for SLA monitoring focuses on statistical delay thresholds.

üìè Refined Method:

  1. Keep steps 1‚Äì4 from the basic SLA measurement procedure (e.g., sending test packets, collecting delay data).
  2. Define \(N^*\) as the number of packets whose delay exceeds the allowed threshold \(d\).
  3. Calculate the fraction of violations: \[ \frac{N^*}{N} \] where \(N\) is the total number of test packets sent.
  4. Compare this ratio to the agreed SLA probability \(p\).

üß† If:

\[ \frac{N^*}{N} > p \]

then the SLA has been violated.

üéØ Why is this better?

  • Focuses on how often delays exceed the limit, not just the average.
  • Aligns perfectly with statistical SLAs that define acceptable levels of performance violations.
  • Prevents both client and provider from hiding behind averages or rare spikes.
